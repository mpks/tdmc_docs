Holstein model gapÔÉÅ

The gap for the Holstein model at the zero temperature can be computed numerically. Starting from the Holstein Hamiltonian

\[\hat{H} = -\gamma \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \hat{c}_{i}^\dagger\hat{c}_{i+1} + {\rm h.c.} \right) + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta_i \left( \hat{c}_{i}^\dagger\hat{c}_{i} - 1/2 \right) + \frac{1}{8\lambda\gamma} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta_i^2 + \frac{\omega_{0}^2}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \tilde{P}_{i}^2.\]

and we assume \(\tilde{P}_i=0\) for every \(i\), with initial state set as \(\Delta_i =(-1)^i\Delta\), we get

\[\hat{H} = -\gamma \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \hat{c}_{i}^\dagger\hat{c}_{i+1} + \hat{c}_{i+1}^\dagger\hat{c}_{i} \right) + \Delta \sum_{i=1}^{N} (-1)^i \hat{c}_{i}^\dagger\hat{c}_{i} + \frac{1}{8\lambda\gamma} N\Delta^2\]

We want to transform this Hamiltonian into the inverse space by transforming the annihilation and creation operators

\[\hat{c}_{m}^\dagger =\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{k} e^{-ikm}\hat{c}_k^\dagger,\]

and

\[\hat{c}_{m} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{k} e^{ikm}\hat{c}_k^\dagger.\]

Assuming \(\gamma\) is real, the first term transforms into

\[-\gamma\frac{1}{N}\sum_{m=1}^{N} \sum_k\sum_{k'} \left( e^{-imk}e^{i(m+1)k'}\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{k} \hat{c}_{k'} + e^{-i(m+1)k}e^{imk'}\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{k} \hat{c}_{k'} \right)\]

or

\[-\gamma\frac{1}{N}\sum_{m=1}^{N} \sum_k\sum_{k'} \left( e^{-im(k-k')}e^{ik'}\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{k} \hat{c}_{k'} + e^{-im(k-k')}e^{-ik}\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{k} \hat{c}_{k'} \right)\]

For large \(N\), the Kronecker delta function can be written as

\[\delta_{kk'} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{m=1}^{N}e^{im(k-k')},\]

where \(k=\frac{2\pi}{N}r\) and \(k'=\frac{2\pi}{N}r'\) and \(r\) and \(r'\) are integers going from \(0\) to \(N-1\). The sums over \(m\) and \(k'\) in the first term disappear after the previous transformation and we get

\[-\gamma \sum_k (e^{ik} + e^{-ik})\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{k} \hat{c}_{k} = - \sum_k (2\gamma\cos k) \hat{c}^{\dagger}_{k} \hat{c}_{k}\]

The second term transforms as

\[\Delta\sum_{m=1}^{N}(-1)^{m}\hat{c}^\dagger_{m}\hat{c}_{m} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{m=1}^{N} \sum_{k}\sum_{k'}e^{-im(k-k')}e^{im\pi}\hat{c}_{k}^\dagger \hat{c}_{k} = \sum_{k}\sum_{k'}\delta_{k,k'+\pi} \hat{c}_{k}^\dagger\hat{c}_{k'} = \Delta\sum_{k} \hat{c}_{k}^\dagger\hat{c}_{k+\pi}\]

Here, the vector \(k\) runs from zero to \(2\pi\) in discrete steps of \(\frac{2\pi}{N}\). Due to the periodicity of the Brillouin zone, and the fact that for \(k\)-s above \(\pi\) we have \(k+\pi \rightarrow k-\pi\), the above sum can be divided into two sums

\[\Delta \left(\sum_{k\le\pi}\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k+\pi} + \sum_{k>\pi}\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k+\pi} \right) = \Delta \left(\sum_{k\le\pi}\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k+\pi} + \sum_{k>\pi}\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k-\pi} \right) =\]
\[\Delta \left(\sum_{k\le\pi}\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k+\pi} + \sum_{k\le\pi}\hat{c}^\dagger_{k+\pi}\hat{c}_{k} \right)\]

In a similar way, assuming \(\epsilon(k)=-2\gamma\cos k\) the sum for the onsite terms can be divided into two parts

\[\sum_{k\le\pi}\epsilon(k)\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k} + \sum_{k>\pi}\epsilon(k)\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k} = \sum_{k\le\pi}\epsilon(k)\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k} + \sum_{k\le\pi}\epsilon(k+\pi)\hat{c}^\dagger_{k+\pi} \hat{c}_{k+\pi} =\]
\[\sum_{k\le\pi}\epsilon(k)\hat{c}^\dagger_{k}\hat{c}_{k} -\sum_{k\le\pi}\epsilon(k)\hat{c}^\dagger_{k+\pi} \hat{c}_{k+\pi}\]

The last sign change comes from the fact that \(\cos(k+\pi) = -\cos k\). Since all sums now run for \(k<\pi\) the Brillouin zone is now reduced and the Hamiltonian can be written as a \(2\times 2\) matrix

\[\begin{split}\sum_{k\le\pi} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{c}_{k}^\dagger & \hat{c}_{k+\pi} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon(k) & \Delta \\ \Delta & -\epsilon(k) \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{c}_{k} \\ \hat{c}_{k+\pi} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

For each \(k\) the matrix can be diagonalized and the eigenenergies can be computed

\[\begin{split}{\rm det} \begin{vmatrix} \epsilon(k) -\lambda_k & \Delta \\ \Delta & -\epsilon(k) - \lambda_k \end{vmatrix} = -(\epsilon(k) - \lambda_k)(\epsilon(k) + \lambda_k) - \Delta^2 = \lambda_k^2 - \epsilon(k)^2 - \Delta^2 = 0.\end{split}\]

From here we get the eigen energies as

\[\lambda_{k,\pm} = \pm \sqrt{\epsilon(k)^2 + \Delta^2}.\]

This corresponds to two bands for each \(k\), and the bands are separated by the gap \(2\Delta\) at \(k = \pi\). The total energy for a half-filled system is then obtained by summing over the lower band and adding the extra constant

\[E_{\rm tot} = -\sum_{k\le\pi} \sqrt{\epsilon(k)^2 + \Delta^2} + \frac{N\Delta^2}{8\lambda\gamma}\]

The band gap \(\Delta\) for a given coupling \(\lambda\) can be determined by minimizing the derivative of total energy \(\partial E_{\rm tot} /\partial \Delta\) with respect to \(\Delta\).

\[\frac{\partial E_{\rm tot}}{\partial \Delta} = -\sum_{k<\pi} \frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{\epsilon(k)^2 + \Delta^2}} + \frac{N\Delta}{4\lambda\gamma} = 0\]

From here we get two solutions for the gap, one is \(\Delta = 0\) which we discard. The other is

\[\sum_{k\le\pi} \frac{1}{N} \frac{4\lambda\gamma}{\sqrt{\epsilon(k)^2 + \Delta^2}} - 1 = 0\]

The gap is computed numerically. We set the upper and lower limit for the gap \(\Delta_{\rm max}\) and \(\Delta_{\rm min}\) and compute the derivative using the previous equation. If the derivative is negative, the \(\Delta\) needs to get smaller (because the derivative scales as \(\frac{1}{\Delta}\)), on the other end, if the derivative is positive, the \(\Delta\) needs to increase. By moving the limits \(\Delta_{\rm min}\) and \(\Delta_{\rm max}\) we compute the \(\Delta\) which gives the zero of the first energy derivative.
